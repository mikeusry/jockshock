## Executive answer

JockShock is a viable business. The realistic path is **DTC + B2B-led, with heavy-use behavior change as the load-bearing strategic mechanism.** No Amazon (Mike's hard rule). Position as **premium pro-grade**, not value play.

The single biggest tradeoff: optimize for efficient profit conversion (DTC + B2B with productized sales motion) vs. brand-building at higher CAC and slower payback. Mike's profit-over-revenue philosophy points toward the first.

---

## Format strategy

**Single 32oz format, 1/3/6 quantity ladder.** Don't add an 8oz trial size in v1. Reconsider only after channel mix data exists.

- 32oz reads as "serious" / "pro" / "family bottle for all the gear in the house" — that frame expands TAM rather than narrowing it, IF the messaging carries it
- A pure value-led message ("4× the bottle") commoditizes the brand. Lead with HOCl performance + routine; close with cost-per-use as proof point
- Force of Nature is the cleanest analog: HOCl, premium-positioned, "80% less per ounce" as supporting argument not headline

**Six-pack consumer SKU is mismatched.** 6 × 32oz = 1.5 gallons is facility-shaped, not household-shaped. Reposition as B2B intake (quote form), keep purchasable on backend. True B2B SKUs (gallons, 5-gallon pails) come after demand validates.

## CAC + acquisition

Specific CAC benchmarks for "sports gear deodorizer" are thin. Triangulating from <$60 AOV DTC consumables and athletic accessories in 2025–2026:

- **Meta:** $25–$50 per first purchase with competent creative
- **TikTok:** $20–$45; cheaper with viral UGC, higher variance
- **Google Shopping/Search:** $25–$60, more stable due to intent ("sports gear disinfectant," "football pad spray")
- **Conversion rate** to single-product PDP at $24.99 from cold paid: 1–3% baseline, 3–5% with sharp positioning

For a low-awareness category like this:
- Problem/solution leads outperform value-led ads at top-of-funnel ("kill the hockey-bag smell" beats "4× the bottle")
- Time to first profitable cohort: 2–4 months at modest scale
- Owned content/SEO ("how to clean hockey gear," "lacrosse pad smell") becomes a real channel without Amazon backstop

## Retention + heavy-use strategy

**Untrained-buyer cadence:** 32oz lasts a single athlete 4–8 months. 6-month repeat: 10–25%. 12-month: 25–35%. LTV barely clears CAC.

**Trained-buyer cadence (Mike's strategy):** spray after every practice/game → 32oz lasts 6–10 weeks. 6-month repeat could hit 40–60%. LTV roughly 2× untrained.

**Training adoption rate is the load-bearing variable:**
| Adoption | Avg orders/buyer/yr | Yr 1 contribution (base CAC/spend) |
|---|---|---|
| 20% (low) | 1.4 | -$8K |
| 25% (realistic v1) | 1.7 | $20K |
| 40% (planning case) | 2.2 | $55K |
| 60% (stretch) | 3.1 | $215K |

**The Klaviyo flow IS the product mechanism.** Standard replenishment ("reorder reminder at month 4") is wrong. Right shape: behavior reinforcement — day 3 routine setup, day 14 check-in, day 30 gear-bag check, day 60 running-low. Subscription default cadence: 60 days, not 6 months.

Without trained heavy use, the no-Amazon model breaks. With training at even 25–40%, unit economics work.

## Channel mix — DTC + B2B only (no Amazon, no big-box retail Y1)

**Year 1 base case:** ~70% DTC / ~30% B2B.

**B2B must be productized**, not Mike-driven. 10-hr/wk cap rules out a sales-heavy motion. System handles 80%:
- One-page intake form (Shopify/Tally/Typeform)
- Automated 3-email follow-up sequence
- Pre-priced "Gym Starter Kit" SKU (3×32oz + branded carrying case + reorder card)
- Sample fulfillment workflow with Klaviyo segmentation
- Mike notification rule: only ping when org >50 members OR sample-receiver hasn't ordered in 14 days

**Why no Amazon (Mike's hard rule):** 2015–2017 Biggle Bird experience. Grew to thousands of bottles/month, #1 poultry product, then Amazon raised fees to ~45% of revenue, leaving <5% margin. Mike measures success in profit not GMV. Amazon hides the customer relationship and can change terms unilaterally. Closed decision — never re-propose.

## Top 5 structural risks (ranked)

1. **32oz reads as "only for pros," hurting DTC conversion.** Indicator: high PDP view → ATC drop-off, "too big" feedback. Mitigation: reframe as "family-size bottle, one bottle for all your kids' gear," add cost-per-season math.

2. **Heavy-use training fails to land.** Indicator: replenishment-flow open/click rates trend down after 30 days, 6-month cohort repeat <15%. Mitigation: shift cross-sell focus to adjacent SKUs (laundry additive, gym wipes) so LTV doesn't rest on bottle replenishment.

3. **B2B execution stalls without productized system.** Indicator: intake form submissions <5/month by month 3, Mike's calendar consumed by sales calls. Mitigation: dedicated VA/agency to run the intake-to-sample-to-close pipeline against the system Mike defined.

4. **Free shipping on multi-packs eats margin.** 6-pack ships ~12.5 lbs; absorbed shipping is $14–22. Indicator: shipping cost/order rises above plan. Mitigation: threshold-based free ship (≥$49), packaging optimization, B2B orders move to quoted shipping.

5. **Compliance/claim missteps.** "24HR Protection," "Clinical Strength," "Pro Grade," "D1 Approved" all carry FTC/EPA risk. Indicator: aggressive claims in ads without matched label data. Mitigation: tight legal review, align with EPA-approved language, substantiate or remove "D1 Approved" before paid launches.

## Comparable brands (verified May 2026)

- **Clear Gear** — direct format competitor at 32oz / $19.95 / $0.62/oz on Amazon. 1K+ bought past month, 4.4 stars, 745 reviews. EPA-registered. Visually low-rent. Strong B2B (schools, gyms, athletic programs) + Amazon presence. Mike won't compete on Amazon — but Clear Gear's footprint there shows category demand exists.
- **Vapor Fresh** — plant-based, 4oz/16oz/multi-packs. Strong B2B (gyms, facilities) + retail store-locator. Decade+ in market. ~$0.94/oz at 16oz.
- **2Toms StinkFree (Medi-Dyne)** — 8oz at $11.99 ($1.50/oz). Distributed via running specialty stores, Amazon, Medi-Dyne DTC.
- **Defunkify** — enzyme detergent + booster, no gear spray. ~$3M revenue (RocketReach, unverified). DTC + Amazon + retail. Activewear/outdoor positioning.
- **Force of Nature** — HOCl device + capsule subscription. Premium-positioned, "80% less per ounce" closing argument. Best analog for HOCl marketing/claims handling and behavior-driven subscription mechanics.
