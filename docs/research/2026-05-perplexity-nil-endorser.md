## The softball question (go/no-go and framing)

### Has anyone made "stink/sweat/grit" positive for women?

- Major women's-sport campaigns from Nike, Under Armour's "I Will What I Want," and Adidas "She Breaks Barriers" explicitly lean into sweat, effort, and physicality as empowering, not shameful.
- Deodorant/personal care brands have moved toward "whole-body" and sweat-positive messaging, often targeting women first; Lume's growth and major CPG entrants into full-body deodorant show consumer acceptance of frank odor talk if framed as care and confidence rather than embarrassment.

This suggests the "girls don't want to be associated with stink" concern is more vibes than data — modern campaigns show women respond well to honesty about sweat when it connects to empowerment, confidence, and performance.

### Recommendation: Go, with carefully controlled creative.

Softball has:
- Strong SEC footprint and parent-of-athlete overlap.
- Heavy, re-used gear (bags, helmets, catcher's gear) that truly stinks, making the product story real, not contrived.
- Cultural positioning around toughness, blue-collar work ethic, and "plays like baseball but for my daughter," which resonates with sports moms and dads.

### Creative framing that works

Principles:
- Never say "you smell"; say "your gear works as hard as you do."
- Make stink an outcome of **earned** effort and a threat to readiness, not femininity.

Concept angles:

1. **Earned effort → next-day ready**
   - Line: "If my bag doesn't stink, I didn't work hard enough. That's why I clean it after every practice so I'm ready for tomorrow."
   - Visual: Athlete unzipping a bag, quick grin at the camera, misting gear while talking about routine (recovery shake, stretch, spray the gear).

2. **Protect the gear, protect the season**
   - Line: "My glove and catcher's gear have been with me for years. JockShock keeps them fresh so my focus stays between the lines."
   - Emphasis: Care for equipment as professionalism; this also resonates with parents who invest in expensive bats/bags.

3. **Mom-co-sign narrative**
   - Short reel with athlete + her mom:
     - Mom: "The smell from that softball bag used to knock me over."
     - Athlete: "Now cleaning it is just part of my after-practice routine."
   - This directly addresses the buyer (mom) while keeping the athlete aspirational, not "gross."

4. **Team ritual**
   - "After every practice, before we leave the locker room, we wipe down and spray our gear. It's just what our program does."
   - Aim for the "this is what serious teams do" B2B halo — not an individual personal-odor storyline.

Guardrails:
- Avoid copy like "stop smelling bad" or "girls can stink too."
- Avoid zooming in on body odor; focus on gear, bags, lockers, and team spaces.
- Let the athlete set the tone; if she naturally jokes about stink, keep it light and pride-driven.

**Net:** Softball is a go if you make it about pride, professionalism, and gear care, not about "fixing" female odor.

## Single hero vs. micro-roster vs. hybrid

### What DTC brands are actually doing in NIL

- Platforms like NIL Club and Postgame emphasize scale: brands use dozens or hundreds of micro-deals to generate content and reach, often at low four-figure budgets per athlete or less.
- CeraVe's NIL push used a wide roster (100+ athletes) to generate 250–300 pieces of social content, showing how skin/hygiene brands prefer breadth and routine-content over a single hero in NIL.
- ZIPS Car Wash ran a multi-school NIL initiative with multiple athletes creating "Car Wash Convos," blending local presence with many small NIL deals rather than a single expensive face.

These patterns indicate that in the $10K–$30K range, **brands win with breadth plus tight creative briefs**, not one large bet.

### For JockShock's $5K–$25K envelope

- **Single hero ($20K):**
  - Pros: Strong narrative cohesion, easier creative control, can build a mini-docu-series on routine.
  - Cons: Risk concentration; if the athlete under-performs or has issues, the spend is sunk; limited reach across sports/segments; harder B2B leverage.

- **Micro-roster (4–5 × $3K–$5K):**
  - Pros: More shots on goal, cross-sport proof ("used by athletes from gymnastics to softball"), more UGC content for your channels, ability to test which sport/angle actually moves DTC and B2B.
  - Cons: More coordination, shallower stories per athlete unless you standardize content formats.

- **Hybrid (anchor ~ $8K–$10K + 2–3 × $2K–$3K):**
  - Pros: Maintains a flagship storyteller (e.g., SEC gymnast or softball captain) while testing secondary sports (wrestling, women's basketball) and avoiding full concentration risk.
  - Cons: Still some coordination overhead, though manageable.

### Recommendation: hybrid

For Year 1, run a hybrid:
- 1 anchor ($8K–$10K) in a high-routine, high-trust archetype (e.g., women's gymnastics or softball at a major SEC program).
- 2–3 micros ($2K–$3K each) across softball, wrestling, and women's basketball or track to test creative angles and attribution.

This mirrors the "anchor + micro-influencer squad" pattern common in DTC influencer marketing and aligns with CeraVe/Postgame-style diversified NIL without needing a 50-athlete roster.

## Coaches and program-level endorsements

### Legality and norms

- NIL rules cover athletes, but schools can separately sell sponsorship inventory like "official product of [school athletics]" through their multimedia rights partners (LEARFIELD, Playfly, etc.).
- The ZIPS Car Wash deal: ZIPS signed a multi-school sponsorship with LEARFIELD plus co-branded NIL deals with individual athletes for "Car Wash Convos," showing a legal structure combining program sponsorship and athlete NIL.
- Strength-and-conditioning coaches and equipment managers at public universities are typically employees; endorsements may be restricted by school policy and conflict rules; some schools allow sponsored content if structured as institutional sponsorship rather than personal influencer-style deals.

### Viability and cost

- Full "official product of [Power-5 program]" packages can range from mid-five-figures into six-figures depending on rights and exposure; even smaller conference sponsorships usually exceed a $25K total Year 1 spend when rights fees, signage, and creative are included.
- Lower-tier or Olympic-sport "official partner" status might be negotiable in the low- to mid-five-figure range at non-blue-blood programs, but this is still likely to eat your entire Year 1 budget.

### High-ROI structures for JockShock

Given Year 1 constraints:

- **Focus on informal but documented program usage + earned media** rather than big-ticket rights. Example structure:
  - Provide free or discounted product to strength coaches/equipment managers.
  - Create behind-the-scenes social content through athletes showing "our team sprays down gear after every practice."
  - Ask the program for permission to mention "[School] athletes use JockShock" in B2B pitches without formal "official" language (subject to compliance).

- **Explore low-cost, sport-specific partnerships:** e.g., "Official gear deodorizer of [School] women's gymnastics" through a small Olympic-sports sponsorship or booster-club deal, which may sit in the $5K–$15K band at some schools; this must be cleared with the school's rights holder and compliance office.

**Net:** Traditional "official partner" deals are likely too capital-intensive in Year 1, but a combination of NIL athletes plus quiet program trials and small sport-level sponsorships can give B2B proof points at far better ROI.

## Top 3 NIL archetypes to scout (for ~$15K total)

All described, not named, and tuned for SEC-region preference.

### 1. SEC Women's Gymnastics Leader (Anchor)
- **Profile:** Upper-class gymnast or former all-around leader at a major SEC program with strong Instagram/YouTube presence and a "big sister" vibe for youth gymnasts and their parents.
- **Why:** Gym is the ultimate routine sport; chalk, mats, and leos are all hygiene-sensitive; parents already obsess over cleanliness and safety; female gymnasts have shown strong marketing power.
- **Budget slot:** $8K–$10K for a full-season collab with 6–10 structured content pieces + appearances at 1–2 regional activations.

### 2. SEC Softball Everyday Starter (Co-anchor or Micro)
- **Profile:** Starting shortstop/catcher/outfielder at a top-15 SEC softball program, known as a grinder and leader, with solid but not celebrity-level social following and strong local/regional visibility.
- **Why:** Direct line from her to your softball-family buyer; gear stink is obvious; routine post-practice cleaning is easy to film and emulate; fits SEC-region in-person activation strategy.
- **Budget slot:** $3K–$5K.

### 3. Wrestling or Women's Basketball Culture-Carrier (Micro)
- **Wrestling option:** 149–184 lb starter at a prominent D1 program within a drivable radius of Georgia, respected for toughness and regimen; strong mat-hygiene tie.
- **Women's basketball option:** Starting guard at an SEC or adjacent program, visible to families and youth players, with a "details and discipline" persona.
- **Why:** Both sports scream routine, repetition, and sweat; both have locker-room and B2B influence that extends to HS/club programs.
- **Budget slot:** 2 × $2K–$3K for two athletes across these sports.

This structure roughly yields a $15K hybrid: 1 gymnast anchor plus 2–3 micros across softball, wrestling, and women's basketball/track.

## Brand-safety and contract checklist

NIL-specific compliance platforms and industry guidance emphasize clear, written contracts, disclosure, and school compliance filings.

**Must-have clauses:**

1. **Morals/behavior clause**
   - May terminate or pause the agreement if the athlete:
     - Commits or is charged with serious crimes.
     - Engages in hate speech, harassment, discriminatory conduct, or explicit illegal drug promotion online.
     - Creates content reasonably likely to bring substantial public disrepute on JockShock.

2. **Content approval rights**
   - Require pre-approval for any posts where the brand is tagged, mentioned by name, or logo is visible, with a clear SLA (e.g., 48-hour review window).
   - Allow takedowns of posts where JockShock appears.

3. **Exclusivity / category conflict**
   - Athlete agrees not to endorse competing deodorizer/gear-cleaning brands during the contract + 30–60 days post-term.
   - Clarify they may still wear/use school-issued products as required by team contracts.

4. **Deliverables schedule and usage rights**
   - Define exact content obligations: e.g., 6 Instagram posts, 4 TikToks, 1 longform "routine" video, 1 in-person appearance.
   - Secure perpetual (or multi-year) rights for JockShock to reuse content in paid and organic channels, within limits of NCAA and school rules.

5. **Compliance and disclosure**
   - Athlete must:
     - Disclose and register the deal with the school's NIL/compliance system (e.g., NIL Go) before any deliverables.
     - Follow FTC advertising guidelines (#ad, #sponsored).
   - Contract is void if the deal is ruled non-compliant by the school or state, with pro-rated refunds/relief.

6. **Performance and termination**
   - Non-performance penalties or fee reductions for missed deliverables.
   - "Without cause" termination right for JockShock with notice plus pro-rated payment based on completed work.

7. **Data sharing and attribution rights**
   - Permission to track link clicks, discount code redemptions, and campaign analytics and to share aggregated performance data publicly (without disclosing the athlete's private info).

There are no standard differences in clauses for men's vs. women's sports; risk profiles may differ by sport and individual, but contract paper can be identical across genders.

## Measurement playbook (DTC attribution)

For JockShock, run:

1. **Unique athlete discount code**
   - E.g., "GYMNAST20" for 20% off first order.
   - Pros: Explicit incentive, straightforward tracking.
   - Measure: Orders, revenue, and contribution margin from each code over 14 and 60 days.

2. **Custom landing pages + UTMs**
   - Each athlete gets a vanity URL redirect (e.g., `/gymnast`) pointing to a personalized landing page with:
     - That athlete's photos and quote.
     - Routine framing ("post-practice gear reset in 30 seconds").
   - Every link they share uses UTMs marking athlete + channel; track sessions, add-to-cart, purchases in Shopify.

3. **Affiliate-style structure (NIL-compliant)**
   - NIL allows performance-based compensation as long as it's tied to real work and compliant with state/school rules.
   - Structure: small base fee + per-order bonus (e.g., $2–$4 per first-time order using their code/URL), capped per contract.
   - This encourages athletes to promote over time without up-front overpayment.

4. **Time-windowed tests**
   - For major posts (launch video, in-season feature), define a primary attribution window (e.g., 14 days) and a secondary halo window (up to 60 days) to:
     - Calculate CAC per athlete = (fee + fulfilled bonuses) / new customers attributed.
     - Compare contribution margin vs. $32 blended CAC benchmark.

5. **Controlled creative tests**
   - Use near-identical scripts across athletes in different sports to isolate "sport" effect from "creative" effect (e.g., same 30-second gear-routine format).
   - Compare which sports deliver higher code redemption and repeat-order rate after 8–12 weeks.

Look for at least breakeven or better CAC vs. current channels to justify scaling NIL in Year 2.

## In-region SEC bias: does it really pay off?

- Brands like ZIPS Car Wash selected specific states (Arkansas, UNC, Tennessee) for NIL activation based on store market concentration, not national reach, underlining the ROI of regional alignment.
- NIL platforms emphasize geographic coverage and local relevance as key advantages of working with many college athletes; brands can pick athletes near their physical or sales markets for activations and earned media.

For JockShock:

A $5K SEC-region athlete gives:
- In-person content (locker-room demos, local gym activations, HS/club visits) you can film and reuse.
- Local press angles ("Georgia-based brand partners with [UGA/SEC] athlete on hygiene innovation").
- Natural B2B wedges into local HS, club, and college strength programs.

Versus a $10K non-regional athlete:
- More social reach, but lose:
  - Physical activation.
  - Regional credibility and word-of-mouth.
  - Ease of building a dense B2B beachhead in Georgia/adjacent states.

JockShock is likely to generate **higher blended ROI from a $5K in-region SEC athlete than a $10K distant star**, especially when B2B program adoption and downstream referrals are included, even if raw social impressions are lower.

## "Elena Arenas"-type archetype (gymnast at major SEC school)

Without treating any one athlete as a recommendation, the archetype Mike described — top-tier SEC gymnast with strong social presence and "role-model for young girls" positioning — scores extremely high for JockShock:

- **Routine and discipline:** Gymnastics is synonymous with strict routines, repetition, and obsessive attention to detail — perfect for anchoring "after every practice" message.
- **Cleanliness and safety:** Mats, beams, grips, and leos all carry hygiene concerns; parents already think about cleanliness in this sport more than most.
- **Parent trust:** Gymnastics audiences skew heavily toward parent-of-youth-athlete, especially moms; a well-spoken SEC gymnast reads as a safe, aspirational role model.
- **Brand fit:** Aesthetic-driven, high-performance, female-empowerment brand stories are already common in women's sports marketing, so "pro-grade deodorizer for real athletes" fits that environment.

Relative to other archetypes:
- Compared to softball: gymnastics has slightly less overlap with travel-ball parents overall but higher perceived discipline and "cleanliness" narrative.
- Compared to women's basketball: gymnastics has more routine and hygiene parallels; women's hoops has broader cultural buzz but somewhat more fan-driven and less parent-driven audiences.
- Compared to football/baseball: far stronger brand safety and alignment with premium, detail-oriented positioning.

This gymnast-archetype lands in the top 3 for Year 1, alongside a high-credibility SEC softball starter and a wrestling or women's basketball routine-obsessed leader.

## Summary recommendations (pulled together)

- **Sports priority:** Start with women's gymnastics (anchor), then softball, wrestling, and women's basketball/track as micros; treat baseball as a B-tier expansion once creative is dialed.
- **Softball:** It's a go, provided stink is framed as earned effort and the story is about gear/ritual, not personal shame.
- **Structure:** Run a hybrid NIL structure in Year 1 (~$15K–$20K): 1 gymnast-type anchor + 2–3 micros (softball, wrestling, women's basketball/track).
- **B2B:** Use NIL to seed "our team uses this in the locker room" content and to open doors with coaches/equipment staff; avoid large "official partner" rights deals until you scale.
- **Measurement:** Require unique codes, athlete-specific landing pages with UTMs, and an affiliate-style bonus layer, and evaluate each athlete versus $32 blended CAC over 14- and 60-day windows.
